Q1 · 25 Marks

Identify and discuss five (5) forces affecting HRM goals and practices.

// Answer — Unit 2: Trends in HRM

  • 1
    Globalization: Organisations are no longer constrained by borders. HR must ensure employees have cultural adaptability and are ready for global assignments.
    Example: A Zambian bank opening in DRC needs HR to recruit French-speaking staff.
  • 2
    Workforce Diversity: The workforce is heterogeneous (gender, race, religion, disability). HR must shift from treating everyone alike to recognising individual differences and avoiding discriminatory practices.
    Example: Providing flexible work arrangements for different family needs.
  • 3
    Technological Changes: Technology changes the nature of jobs and how HR functions (HRIS). HR must recruit tech-savvy employees and use online/web-based training.
    Example: Using LinkedIn to recruit instead of newspaper ads.
  • 4
    Demographic Trends: Changes in the external labour market (aging population, education levels) affect the internal labour force. HR must know who is available.
    Example: Zambia's youthful population means HR focuses on graduate recruitment.
  • 5
    Economic Trends: Inflation, recession, and unemployment impact HR decisions. In bad economies, HR does "rightsizing" (layoffs/furloughs).
    Example: During COVID-19, HR had to furlough workers due to budget cuts.
Q2 · 15 Marks

Explain the 5 steps of the Human Resource Planning Process.

// Answer — Unit 4, Pages 11–20

  • 1
    Analysis of Organisational Plans & Objectives: HR planning starts with the company's overall strategy. If the objective is expansion, HR must initiate quick recruitment to support new operations.
  • 2
    Assessing Current Human Resources: Creating a profile of current employees using an HRIS (Human Resource Information System). The inventory includes skills, education, performance ratings, and languages spoken.
  • 3
    Determining Demand for Labour (Forecasting): Projecting future HR needs based on projected turnover, retirements, new products, technology changes, and competition in the market.
  • 4
    Forecasting Future Labour Supply: Determining where candidates will come from — Internal Supply: current employees via promotions/transfers. External Supply: labour market, universities, recruitment agencies.
  • 5
    Matching Labour Demand and Supply: Comparing forecasts to pinpoint shortages (→ hire/transfer) or overstaffing (→ layoffs/natural attrition).
Q3 · 15 Marks

Discuss the components of an employee health and safety programme.

// Answer — Unit 3 (Safe Workplace) & Unit 2 (Maintenance Function)

  • 1
    Safe Working Conditions: Ensuring the physical environment is free from hazards — noise, heat, dust, and dangerous equipment as outlined in the Job Description (section 6).
  • 2
    Employee Assistance Programs (EAP): Helping employees deal with personal issues (substance abuse, child care, depression) because personal problems directly affect productivity and workplace safety.
  • 3
    Safety Training: Properly training employees on how to use equipment safely and avoid careless habits that lead to accidents and injuries.
  • 4
    Health & Safety Committees: Involving employees in safety decisions, incident reporting, and continuous improvement of safety protocols.
  • 5
    Medical & Workers' Compensation: Providing medical insurance and fully complying with compensation laws for work-related injuries under the Workers' Compensation Act.
Q4 · 10 Marks

Identify and discuss any three (3) major equal employment opportunity laws and regulations in Zambia.

// Answer — Unit 3, Pages 4–6

  • 1
    Constitution of Zambia (Article 23): The supreme law of the land. Defines "discrimination" as affording different treatment based on race, tribe, sex, place of origin, political opinions, or creed. All other laws must be consistent with it.
  • 2
    Employment Code Act No. 3 of 2019 — Section 5: Places a positive obligation on the employer to actively promote equal opportunity and eliminate discrimination in recruitment, training, promotion, and termination of employment.
  • 3
    Industrial and Labour Relations Act (Chapter 269): Lists specific prohibited grounds for discrimination including: colour, nationality, pregnancy, marital status, disability, and social origin. This is the law that would directly cover Pamela's case.

Q6 · 25 Marks

Explain the Five (5) elements of a High Performance Work System (HPWS).

// Answer — Industrial Relations PDF, Pages 38–41

  • 1
    Organisational Structure: How the company groups people into divisions and establishes reporting relationships. Flat structures replace tall hierarchies to speed up decisions.
    Example: A marketing team reports directly to the CEO instead of through 4 layers of management.
  • 2
    Task Design: How work activities are grouped into jobs or team responsibilities, enabling workers to see the full picture of their contribution.
    Example: Allowing a team to build and deliver a complete product rather than each member just assembling one component.
  • 3
    People (Selection): Emphasis on hiring employees who are well-suited to their roles and well-prepared for challenges. Employees may participate in selecting new team members.
    Example: Team members interview and vote on candidates before final HR approval.
  • 4
    Reward Systems: Rewards are tied to company financial performance (pay-for-performance model). Individual and team bonuses linked to measurable outcomes.
    Example: Bonuses awarded when a team meets quarterly safety or productivity goals.
  • 5
    Information Systems: Modern IT allows wide, real-time sharing of information across the entire organisation, reducing information silos.
    Example: All employees can see live customer satisfaction scores on a shared dashboard.
HPWS Outcomes: High product quality · Low turnover · Job satisfaction · Constant performance feedback loop
Q7 · 5 Marks

Briefly explain any five (5) causes of an industrial dispute.

// Answer — Industrial Relations PDF, Pages 17–18

  • 1
    Industrial Factors: Disputes over wages, working hours, and employment terms — the most common root cause of industrial action.
  • 2
    Management Attitude: Management's unwillingness to recognise a trade union or engage in meaningful dispute resolution creates adversarial relationships.
  • 3
    Communication Breakdowns: Misunderstandings between managers and workers due to poor, infrequent, or one-directional information flow.
  • 4
    Unreasonable Standards: Unfair rules, arbitrary policies, or burdensome procedures that frustrate employees and erode trust in management.
  • 5
    Unrealised Expectations: Dissatisfaction accumulates when what employees were promised or expected (promotion, pay rise, benefits) does not materialise.
